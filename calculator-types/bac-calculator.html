How BAC Is Calculated — The Widmark Formula

The Widmark formula is the internationally accepted method for estimating blood alcohol concentration: BAC = (alcohol consumed in grams) ÷ (body weight in grams × distribution ratio r) − (elimination rate × hours). The distribution ratio r accounts for body water content — approximately 0.68 for males and 0.55 for females, reflecting differences in body composition.

What the BAC Levels Mean

At 0.02–0.03% you may feel mild relaxation and slight mood lift. At 0.05–0.08%, coordination begins to deteriorate and reaction time slows — this is the range where most countries set the legal driving limit. At 0.08–0.15%, impairment becomes significant: slurred speech, poor balance, impaired judgement. Above 0.15%, nausea and disorientation are common. Above 0.30%, loss of consciousness is possible. Above 0.40% can be fatal.

How Long Does Alcohol Take to Leave Your System?

The liver metabolises alcohol at a roughly fixed rate of 0.015% BAC per hour — about one standard drink per hour for an average adult. No method — coffee, food, cold showers, exercise — speeds this up. Only time sobers you up.

What is a standard drink?

In the United States, one standard drink contains 14 grams of pure alcohol — equivalent to a 12 oz regular beer (5%), a 5 oz glass of wine (12%), or a 1.5 oz shot of spirits (40%). In Australia and New Zealand, a standard drink contains 10 grams of alcohol, so the same beer equals about 1.4 standard drinks.

Why does food lower BAC?

Food — particularly fats and proteins — slows gastric emptying, which delays alcohol entering the small intestine where it's absorbed into the bloodstream. Food doesn't reduce the total amount of alcohol absorbed, but it spreads absorption over a longer period, resulting in a lower peak BAC.

Is the 0.08% limit the same everywhere?

No. The US, UK, Canada, and Australia use 0.05% or 0.08% limits for standard drivers, but many European countries use 0.05%, and some countries (Sweden, Czech Republic, Hungary) use 0.02% or zero tolerance. Professional drivers and those under 21 often face stricter limits. Always check local laws.
